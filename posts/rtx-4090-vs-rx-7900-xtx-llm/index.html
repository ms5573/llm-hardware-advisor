What These Numbers Mean in Practice

At 7B models, the 4090 delivers a near-conversational experience at 140+ tokens per second. The 7900 XTX at 80–100 tok/s is still usable, but you'll notice the difference in rapid back-and-forth conversations or batch processing tasks.

At 13B models — arguably the sweet spot for quality-versus-speed — the 4090 stays comfortably above 90 tok/s. The 7900 XTX drops to 50–70 tok/s, which starts to feel sluggish for interactive use.

The 30B range is where the gap becomes painful for AMD users. The 4090 fits a 30B Q4_K_M model entirely in VRAM and runs it at 40–50 tok/s. The 7900 XTX often requires partial CPU offloading due to ROCm's less efficient memory management, dragging performance down to 25–35 tok/s.

Why is the gap so large given similar specs? Three reasons:

  1. CUDA optimization: llama.cpp, ExLlama2, and vLLM have years of CUDA-specific tuning. ROCm support is functional but consistently lags behind.
  2. Kernel-level efficiency: NVIDIA's tensor cores are more aggressively optimized for the matrix operations that dominate LLM inference.
  3. Windows ROCm limitations: If you're on Windows, AMD's ROCm stack is significantly more limited. Many optimized inference paths simply aren't available outside Linux.

Model Compatibility: What Actually Fits in 24GB

Both cards share the same 24GB VRAM ceiling, so maximum model capacity looks identical on paper. In practice, ROCm's overhead and mixed-precision handling create real differences.

The 70B Reality Check

Neither card runs 70B models fully in VRAM at Q4 quantization — you'll need approximately 35–40GB for that. Both cards will offload layers to system RAM, and performance tanks to 10–20 tok/s depending on your CPU and RAM speed. The 4090 handles this offloading roughly 20% more efficiently due to better PCIe bandwidth utilization and CUDA-optimized layer management.

If 70B is your primary target, you're better off looking at dual-GPU setups or waiting for 48GB cards. For most users, 30B at Q4 or Q5 is the practical sweet spot that both cards can handle — with the 4090 doing it faster and more reliably.

Context Length Considerations

Longer context windows eat VRAM fast. Running a 13B model at FP16 with a 32K context window will push you right to the 24GB limit on both cards. The 4090's more efficient CUDA memory management gives it slightly more headroom in edge cases, but this is a minor advantage for most users.

The Hidden Costs of AMD

The 7900 XTX's sticker price understates its true cost for many users:

  • Time investment: Expect 5–15 hours getting ROCm properly configured, especially on Linux
  • Troubleshooting overhead: ROCm updates can break inference setups; CUDA rarely does
  • Windows limitations: If you're on Windows, many optimized inference tools simply won't work well with AMD
  • Opportunity cost: Every hour debugging ROCm is an hour not spent on actual projects

These aren't dealbreakers for technically confident Linux users, but they're real costs that don't show up in the price tag.

Frequently Asked Questions

1. Can the RX 7900 XTX run local LLMs on Windows?

Yes, but with significant limitations. ROCm support on Windows is substantially less mature than on Linux. Many optimized inference tools — including some ExLlama2 features and vLLM — either don't work or perform poorly on Windows with AMD GPUs. If you're on Windows, the RTX 4090 is the far safer choice. Linux users get a much better AMD experience, though still slower than CUDA.

2. Does the RTX 4090's higher power draw (450W vs 355W) matter for home use?

It matters at the margins. Running the 4090 at full load costs roughly $0.03–0.05 more per hour in electricity than the 7900 XTX (depending on local rates). Over a year of heavy daily use, that's $100–$180 in additional electricity costs — real money, but unlikely to change the overall value calculation for most users. The 4090's faster inference also means it completes tasks sooner, partially offsetting the higher wattage.

3. Can I run a 70B model on either of these cards?

Both cards can run 70B models at Q4 quantization, but neither can fit the full model in VRAM. You'll need to offload layers to system RAM, which dramatically reduces performance to roughly 10–20 tok/s depending on your CPU and RAM speed. For a better 70B experience, consider a dual-GPU setup or look at 48GB cards like the RTX 6000 Ada. If 70B is your primary use case, neither of these cards is the ideal solution.

4. Is the RTX 4090 worth the premium over the RX 7900 XTX?

For heavy users, yes. The 4090 delivers roughly 2x faster inference, works seamlessly on Windows and Linux, and requires zero ROCm troubleshooting. If you're running LLMs daily for work or development, the productivity gains justify the $700–$900 premium within months. For casual users running inference an hour or two per day on smaller models, the 7900 XTX's savings are harder to dismiss.

5. What's the best quantization format to use on each card?

For both cards, Q4_K_M offers the best balance of quality, speed, and VRAM efficiency for most use cases. On the RTX 4090, Q5_K_M is worth considering for 13B–30B models if you want slightly better output quality without a major speed penalty. On the RX 7900 XTX, stick with Q4_K_M — the card's ROCm overhead makes higher quantizations less practical, and the performance hit is more pronounced than on CUDA.
